<p>Solo websites rarely fail because of one dramatic outage. They weaken because small weekly checks stop happening. A dead form, a stale feed, missing analytics, or a broken internal link is enough to reduce trust long before the owner notices.</p>

<p>Use the <a href="/units/blog-solo-website-operations/">solo website operations unit page</a> as the index for follow-up posts, and keep this checklist tied to the baseline rules in <a href="/blog/solo-website-operations-rules-you-should-fix-first/">the first solo-ops guide</a>.</p>

<h2>1. Keep the routine inside 15 minutes</h2>
<p>If a weekly checklist takes 40 minutes, it will eventually be skipped. A solo maintenance loop should be short enough to survive a busy week.</p>

<h2>2. Check public surfaces before internal cleanup</h2>
<p>Start with what visitors and search engines actually see. Open the home page, latest post, feed, sitemap, and one real form flow before touching lower-priority cleanup.</p>

<image-slot code="002" alt="A weekly solo-site maintenance checklist grouped into page checks, feed checks, form checks, analytics checks, and one repair action."></image-slot>

<h2>3. Use one fixed checklist every week</h2>
<p>A practical weekly loop for a solo site can stay very small.</p>
<ul>
  <li>Open the home page and latest post on desktop and mobile</li>
  <li>Check RSS and sitemap URLs return the expected XML</li>
  <li>Submit the contact form once and confirm delivery</li>
  <li>Confirm analytics still records traffic or page views</li>
  <li>Open one important internal link cluster and fix one stale link</li>
  <li>Confirm the latest deployment did not break static assets or redirects</li>
</ul>

<h2>4. Group checks by operating value</h2>
<p>A useful order is reachability, publishing, measurement, and trust. That sequence keeps you on the highest-risk surface first instead of getting trapped in cosmetic cleanup.</p>

<h2>5. Turn repeated failures into permanent checklist lines</h2>
<p>If the same thing breaks twice, it is no longer a random mistake. Add it to the weekly list. That is how a solo operations loop becomes more stable without becoming bigger.</p>

<h2>What to do first</h2>
<p>Write one checklist you can finish in 15 minutes and run it for two straight weeks. If it still feels too heavy, cut it until it survives. A small checklist that actually runs beats a perfect one that gets skipped.</p>
